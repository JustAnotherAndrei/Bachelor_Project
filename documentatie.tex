% =================== ABSTRACT ================================================
\newpage
\thispagestyle{plain}
\begin{center}
  {\Large \textbf{Abstract}}
\end{center}
\vspace{10pt} 

\noindent

This thesis presents \textbf{Sequre}, an interactive web application, meant for studying the distribution of quantum keys (also known as QKD), a method of exchanging cryptographic keys, whose security comes from the laws of physics, rather than the mathematical hardness of some math problems. 
The platform implements the \textbf{BB84 protocol}, alongside three other QKD-related protocols, allowing the user to execute any of these either as a software simulation, or directly on an IBM quantum processor. Every execution is unfolding bit by bit in browser, accompanied by a security analysis which decides whether the resulted key is secure or not. 
A separate section named \textbf{Challenge Mode} completes the main interface. Within it, the user must identify an attack starting from the behaviour of the protocol, or to configure the system themselves in order to hit a special target. The scope of the project is to make accessible, through an ordinary web browser, a subject that usually requires either a specialized hardware, or a research software.

\noindent

When I began to look into what comes next for cryptography, the answer was always the same: quantum computers will eventually break the schemes we rely on today. The natural move would be shifting to post-quantum algorithms, which are also bases to hard mathematical problems, but which believed to withstand quantic attacks. \textbf{Quantum Key Distribution} (QKD) is a more uncommon answer: it does not patch classical cryptography, but replaces the hypothesis it is built on. Instead of trusting that an adversary cannot solve a hard problem, QKD trusts the laws of physics. That shift in foundation is what made me want to study QKD.

The gap that I wanted to close with my thesis is not theory itself, but the accessibility. Real QKD hardware is well out of reach for most universities, and the  software simulators are either built for researchers or are too simplified to teach something substantial. There was room for an interactive platform, browser-accessible, that walks the user through the entire BB84 pipeline, end-to-end, which can also send the same circuits to the IBM Quantum Hardware when solicited. \textbf{Sequre} is the answer to this gap.

My own experience with QKD started from textbooks and academic papers,
and what stayed with me was rarely a definition. It was the moment a
concept clicked because I saw what changed when I tweaked a parameter.
I wanted a tool where a student can move a slider, watch the bit error
rate climb or plummet, see when a channel goes from secure to compromised, and understand why, without first setting up a python environment or reading
hundreds pages of preliminaries. That is why I decided to build this platform.

What follows are the steps of how that platform was built: the protocols
it implements, the design choices behind the simulation engine, the
post-processing layer that turns noisy measurements into a usable key 
and the eavesdropper-detection mechanisms that put the security claims
to the test. The chapters that follow explain why each piece had to be
where it is.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}


Cryptography is the practice of developing and using coded algorithms to protect and obscure transmitted information so that it may only be read by those with the permission and ability to decrypt it. In practice, cryptography is the "invisible shield" of the digital world. It transforms readable data into a long string of characters (ciphered text). 

If we put aside the math behind it, cryptography fulfills four essential functions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Confidentiality}: It ensures that nobody besides the two communicating parties can read any message;
  \item \textbf{Integrity}: Guarantess that the data was neither modified nor truncated while it was transferred;
  \item \textbf{Authentification}: It proves that the site or entity you want to communicate with is actually who they pretend to be;
  \item \textbf{Non-repudation}: It ensures that someone who sent a message or has signed a document cannot deny this fact afterwards.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Cryptographic algorithms} are mathematical procedures and sets of logical rules that are used for securing information by transforming readable data into secret codes, guaranteeing their confidentiality, integrity and authenticity.
Cryptographic algorithms are classified in three categories. Firstly, there are symmetric algorithms, who use a single secret key to quickly encrypt and decrypt large amounts of data, where both the sender and the receiver use the same secret key to "hide" the data, but also to reveal the data; some well-known symmetric algorithms are AES, ChaCha20 and 3DES. Then there are the asymmetric algorithms, the ones using a pair of keys (public and private) in order to securely exchange secret keys and to apply digital signatures; some widely-used asymmetric algorithms are RSA, ECC and Diffie-Hellman. Lastly, there are hash functions, that transform any amount of data into a fixed-length string (a one-way, unique and digital signature) , used only to verify the integrity of data and that of passwords; some popular hashing functions are SHA-256 (section~\ref{sec:ec-pa}), SHA-3 and MD5/SHA-1. A few cryptographic algorithms to keep in mind are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{AES} (Advanced Encryption Standard) \cite{aes2001}: It is the most widely used symmetric algorithm in the world, famous for its speed and extreme efficiency, being the global standard, used by various governments, banks and applications (e.g. Whatsapp) to secure confidential data;

    \item \textbf{RSA} (Rivest-Shamir-Adleman) ~\cite{rsa1978}: This is the pioneer of asymmetric cryptography (with both public and private keys), based on the mathematical difficulty of factorizing big prime numbers, being the algorithm allowed the securization of the first transactions and internet connections;

    \item \textbf{ECC} (Eliptic Curve Cryptography) ~\cite{ecc1985}: It's an asymmetric algorithm that uses the mathematical properties eliptic curves in order to offer the same level of security as RSA, but with way smaller keys, which makes it ideal for smartphones and limited-resource devices.
\end{itemize}

Classic cryptography is based on a security that is purely mathematical and also temporary, as it protects data through complex mathematical problems that current technology finds difficult to solve, leaving systems vulnerable to new algorithms or the inevitable rise of quantum computers, which will be able to instantly crack foundational standards like RSA or ECC using Shor's algorithm. Additionally, it suffers from the challenge of securely distributing secret keys and the absolute impossibility of detecting interceptions in real time, which allows hackers to passively copy and store encrypted data today to decrypt it later, without the sender or receiver ever finding out.

The transition from classical cryptography to \textbf{quantum cryptography} shifts the digital security from the mathematical barriers to the laws of physics. While the classical systems are based on complex equations which quantum computers will eventually crack sometime in the future, quantum cryptography is the science that deals with securing the data through by encoding the information into quantum states of the light particles (photons). This technology uses properties like superposition and quantum inseparability, guaranteeing that the state of said particles is going to modify at any intercepting attempt.

In practice, this phenomenon offers a very high security and introduces an unique "alarm system": any cyber attack will leave a fingerprint that is instantly detectable. Therefore, information security does not "depend" on the computing power of an attacker, but becomes a certainty, assured by nature's law, immune in front of any quantum computer.

A \textbf{protocol} is a set of rules which two or more parties have to follow, in order to accomplish a common task. In the context of cryptography, it describes exactly what each party sends, what do each of them receive and what do they do with the information, such that in the end they'll obtain the desired result (e.g. a common key)

\textbf{Sequre} implements four QKD protocols representing two complementary paradigms: \textit{prepare and measure}: the  \textbf{BB84} protocol and its variants, \textbf{B92} and \textbf{SARG04}, as well as \textit{entanglement-based}, the most sensible choice being the \textbf{E91} protocol.  This dual coverage lets the user compare not just the protocol variants, but two fundamentally different routes to quantum key distribution. Each of these protocols illustrates a different aspect of quantum cryptography and can be put head to head within the same platform. \textbf{BB84} is the reference-protocol, created by \textbf{Charles Bennett} (physicist) and \textbf{Gilles Brassard} (computer scientist) in 1984 ~\cite{bb84}, being the first historically one to ever be proposed and also the one that this entire application "revolves" around. The other three are alternatives either extend or modify the original approach. I included them because they allow direct comparisons within the same platform: same experimental conditions, same attacks, same interface, but different internal mechanisms. Thus, my thesis is not limited to only one implementation, but offers a "bird's-eye view" upon the many directions which the quantum cryptography has taken over the past forty years.

The idea behind \textbf{BB84} is simple. Two parties who want to exchange a secret key use quantum particles (photons) as information carriers. One of them encodes each bit of the key into a quantum state of a photon, choosing from several possible bases. The other entity measures every received photon, randomly choosing the base. In the end, both of them publicly announce their bases and only the bits for whom they have chosen \textbf{the same basis}. These bits make up the common key. The protocol's security is not based off a mathematical hypothesis, but on a physics principle. Any attempt of a third party to read the photons through transport will modify their quantum state, leaving a measurable "mark" in the resulting key. 

However, no matter how good it is implemented, a QKD protocol will \textbf{never} produce an absolute perfect key. A portion of those bits may be wrong, either due to the natural noise of the channel or because of an adversarial intervention. To evaluate this, the two parties a small subset of bits from the obtained common key and check how many bits match. The percentage of the bits that do not match is called \textbf{QBER} (short for Quantum Base Error Rate). QBER is the main security measure in QKD. If its value is below a certain threshold, usually set to 11\%~\cite{shorPreskill2000}, then the two entities can conclude that the transmission was secure enough so that they can use the key further; but if it exceeds the threshold, the trnasmission is abandoned and the protocol begins all over again.

Throughout the whole thesis I will be using the standard names from cryptography literature. \textbf{Alice} and \textbf{Bob} are the two legitimate entities that want to exchange a secret key. \textbf{Eve} is the third actor, the attacker who wants to intercept the key on its way, without Alice and Bob noticing. Alice has the role of the sender. She generates the bits randomly and encodes them into quantum states, which are further sent on the quantum channel. Bob has the role of the receiver. He measures the received states and takes part at the necessary processing steps in order to obtain the final key. Eve is able to make any operation that is allowed by the laws of physics: measure the photons in the channel, can replace them with other bits, can store them in the quantum memory or can copy parts of them, when the source allows it. 

Also, we can't have the full picture without understanding how the attacks work. As far as my thesis is concerned, two classes of attacks appear.
The \textbf{intercept-resend} attack is the canonical active attack on BB84: Eve measures every qubit in a base of her choice and forward a fresh one to Bob in the state corresponding to her outcome. Since Eve picks the wrong base half the time, this attack inevitably introduces detectable errors into Bob's key. A full intercept-resend raises the QBER to around $25\%$, which is well above any realistic noise limit.
\textbf{Sequre} parameterizes it as \textbf{Weak}, \textbf{Strong} and \textbf{Smart} Eve, the last one actively keeping the QBER below the detection threshold.
The \textbf{Photon Number Splitting} (PNS) attack is more subtle and specific to imperfect physical sources: real implementation use attenuated lasers that occasionally emmit multi-photon pulses. Eve counts the photon in every pulse, blocks the single-photon ones and keeps a copy of each multi-photon pulse for later measurement, once Alice and Bob announce their bases. PNS leaves the QBER unscathed and would compromise long-distance BB84 entirely if not for the decoy-state defence ~\cite{lo2005decoy,wang2005decoy} (see section\ref{sec:decoy-state}). 

A second principle of quantum mechanics that QKD can use is
\textbf{quantum entanglement}: two particles can be prepared in
a common state such that measuring one instantly fixes the outcome of the other, no matter how far apart they are. This correlation is stronger than anything classical pre-arrangement can ever produce. Alice measures one photon and Bob the
other, and the perfect correlation of their outcomes both yields
the shared bits and certifies that no eavesdropper has disturbed
the pair.


\textbf{Sequre} brings these elements together in a single browser-based platform.
The user is able to choose and then configure a QKD protocol from a sidebar with
seven preset scenarios, such as lab bench, metro link, satellite uplink, adversarial,
as well as one preset per alternative protocol, and a set of free parameters that
include noise sliders, channel distance, photon source type, error-correction method,
and the adversary model (section~\ref{sec:config-panel}). Each run unfolds cell by
cell on a live grid, streamed over a WebSocket connection
(section~\ref{sec:websocket-photongrid}), and the user observes the key being
distilled step by step from raw qubits via sifting, error correction, and privacy
amplification (section~\ref{sec:ec-pa}) to a final 128-bit secret key. Beyond the
four protocols mentioned above, the platform supports execution either on the
Qiskit-Aer noise simulator (section~\ref{sec:qiskit}) or directly on real IBM
Quantum superconducting processors via Qiskit IBM Runtime
(section~\ref{sec:ibm-runtime}). It implements five adversary models: None, Weak,
Strong, Smart, and PNS (introduced in section~\ref{sec:actors}, implemented in
section~\ref{sec:eve-impl}), three layers of analytical depth: optical fibre
attenuation (section~\ref{sec:channel-loss}), decoy-state bounds
(section~\ref{sec:decoy-state}), finite-key bounds
(section~\ref{sec:finite-key-theory}), two machine-learning eavesdropping detectors
(Random Forest and LSTM, section~\ref{sec:ml-detection}), a Bell test panel for E91
(CHSH inequality check, section~\ref{sec:bell-panel}), and a gamified Challenge Mode
with fifteen procedurally generated missions (section~\ref{sec:challenge-impl}),
meant to test the user's knowledge. Authenticated users persist their simulation
history and Challenge Mode progress in a local SQLite database
(section~\ref{sec:db-schema}), with sign in through email w/ password or Google
OAuth (section~\ref{sec:auth-stack}).

This thesis is structured as follows. \textbf{Chapter}~\ref{sec:application-context} states
the problem that \textbf{Sequre} addresses, sketches what the platform does, what goals it has and
introduces the three cryptographic actors (Alice, Bob, Eve) referred to throughout

QuNetSim, SimulaQron, and the Qiskit notebooks) and positions \textbf{Sequre}

technology stack on which the platform is built. \textbf{Chapter}~\ref{sec:architecture}
describes the three-tier application architecture, the backend and frontend module
organisation, the database schema, and the end-to-end request flow.
\textbf{Chapter}~\ref{sec:implementation} walks through every implemented feature panel by

\textbf{Chapter}~\ref{sec:conclusions} summarises the contributions of this work and
outlines directions for future development.


\section{Application Context and Problem Statement}
\label{sec:application-context}

\subsection{The Problem}
\label{sec:problem}

Quantum key distribution has been studied for decades (BB84 has been around since 1984, so definitely has been a long time since) but it
remains hard to learn for most students, and there are two main
reasons why. Firstly, real QKD hardware is very expensive and only
available in a few research labs; a student at an ordinary
university has no realistic way to see the protocols running on
actual qubits. Second, the software tools that simulate QKD fall
into two extremes. On one side, frameworks like \textbf{NetSquid} and
\textbf{QuNetSim} are written for researchers and require setting up a
Python environment, reading a multitude of pages of documentation  and understanding the simulator's internal model before any output can be produced. On the other side, the Qiskit textbook notebooks show a short BB84 example in a few cells, but it stops short of the full protocol: there is no step that corrects the inevitable errors in the shared bits, no step that shrinks the key so an attacker who listened in on the channel cannot reconstruct it, no defence against advanced attacks on a real laser source, no eavesdropper detection beyond a basic error-rate comparison, and no per-qubit visualisation of the exchange.

The result is that a student who wants to understand BB84
from one end to another has to either accept the protocol on faith from a textbook or spend weeks in a tool meant for researchers. Neither path teaches the protocol in the way most people actually learn: by moving a slider, watching what changes, and asking why.

\subsection{What Sequre Does}
\label{sec:what-sequre-does}

\textbf{Sequre} is a web application that walks the user through
the full BB84 pipeline, qubit by qubit, in any modern browser.
The user opens the page, picks a protocol from a sidebar, sets up a few parameters (the qubit count, the noise level, the channel distance, the presence of an eavesdropper), and presses one button. The page then shows each qubit being prepared by Alice, sent across the public channel, and measured by Bob. The error rate on Bob's side, the bits Alice and Bob both kept after comparing their basis choices, the bits that remain after errors are corrected, and the final shortened secret key are reported as they are produced.

In addition to BB84, \textbf{Sequre} also implements three other
QKD protocols: B92, SARG04, and E91. The user can run any of
these on either a software simulator (Qiskit-Aer with a
configurable noise model) or, when IBM Quantum credentials are
provided, on a real superconducting quantum processor (through
the Qiskit IBM Runtime). Same interface, same parameters,
and the same reported metrics are used in both modes.

\subsection{Goals}
\label{sec:goals}

\textbf{Sequre} is built first as a teaching tool. A student
should be able to use it to:

\begin{itemize}
  \item watch how a BB84 run produces a shared key, step by step;
  \item see how the error rate on Bob's side reacts to noise, channel distance, and
        eavesdropping;
  \item compare the four protocols (BB84, B92, SARG04, E91) on
        the same configuration and see what changes;
  \item observe how attacks (Eve measuring each qubit and sending Bob a fresh fake copy, or Eve taking advantage of a real laser source) affect the channel verdict;
  \item watch the defence based on sending pulses at two different intensities catch attacks that a simple error-rate comparison alone would miss;
  \item see how machine-learning detectors flag attackers that
        stay below the error-rate cut-off;
  \item run the same protocol on real IBM hardware and compare
        the result with the simulator.
\end{itemize}

A second goal is to provide a working bench that a developer or
researcher can use to test ideas quickly: change a parameter,
run a batch of simulations, look at the error-rate history or the
feature-importance scores of the machine-learning detector. The
Challenge Mode, described later in this thesis, builds on this
bench by turning it into a small game of configurable missions.

\subsection{The Three Actors}
\label{sec:actors}

Throughout the rest of this thesis the protocols are described
using the three standard cryptographic characters.

\textbf{Alice} is the sender. She holds the qubit source,
generates a random sequence of bits, encodes each bit into a
qubit (in BB84, by picking one of two possible measurement bases at random), and sends the qubits one by one across the
quantum channel. In \textbf{Sequre}, Alice's random choices are
drawn from a pseudo-random number generator that the user can
seed for reproducibility.

\textbf{Bob} is the receiver. He measures each incoming qubit in
a basis of his own random choice, records the outcome, and later
exchanges his basis choices with Alice over a classical public
channel. The bits where Alice and Bob picked the same basis form the shared raw key; the rest are discarded. In \textbf{Sequre}, Bob's
measurement is executed on the Qiskit-Aer simulator or on the
IBM hardware, depending on the mode the user has selected.

\textbf{Eve} is the eavesdropper. She tries to learn the key by
acting on the quantum channel: she can measure each intercepted qubit and send Bob a fresh, fake copy in its place, or, when \textbf{Alice} uses a real laser source instead of an ideal single-photon emitter, she can take advantage of the rare pulses that contain more than one photon (she keeps one photon for herself and lets the others through to Bob). The laws of quantum mechanics make it impossible for Eve to do this without
leaving a trace; the size of that trace is what the rest of the
thesis measures. \textbf{Sequre} exposes five Eve modes: None
(Eve is absent), Weak (Eve intercepts and resends only a fraction of the qubits), Strong (Eve intercepts and resends every qubit), Smart (Eve adapts her behaviour to keep the visible error rate just below the level at which the protocol would abort), and PNS (Eve targets the real laser source by skimming photons from multi-photon pulses).

\newpage
\section{Theoretical Background}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Post-Quantum Cryptography vs QKD}
\label{sec:pqc-vs-qkd}

In 1994, \textbf{Peter Shor} proved that a quantum computer that is large enough could factorize very large integers and solve the \textbf{discrete logarithm problem}~\cite{shor1994}, breaking RSA and ECC. Existing quantum hardware is not yet large enough, but the gap is getting smaller and smaller. Encrypted traffic captured today can be stored and decrypted retroactively once the threshold is reached. This is the \textbf{"harvest now, decrypt later"} concern (i.e. "capture encrypted traffic now, and decrypt it later, once quantum computers exist").

\textbf{Post-quantum cryptography} (PQC) stays in software: it replaces RSA and ECC with new schemes based on harder math problems, standardised by NIST (National Institution of Standards and Technology) in 2024. Its security remains computational, with no proof that the quantum algorithms cannot break the new problems either.

\textbf{Quantum Key Distribution} (QKD) replaces the hypothesis itself. Its security is informational-theoretic and is derived from physics, not from math, but it requires a quantum channel and dedicated hardware. QKD does not replace classical cryptography, instead it distributes the symmetric keys that algorithms like AES then use to encrypt the actual traffic. This chapter develops the QKD route, which is the one \textbf{Sequre} studies. 

What makes both Shor's attack and the protection offered by QKD is the same shift in the below-layer. Information is no longer carried by classic bits, but by \textbf{qubits}, which obey other rules, rules that classical computation simply does not have. The next subsection sets out the necessary \textbf{quantum computing} vocabulary, which it relies on.

\subsection{Quantum Computing Foundations}
\label{sec:qc-foundations}

\subsubsection{Qubits and Quantum States}

The fundamental unit of quantum information is the \textbf{qubit} (short for quantum bit). While a classical bit can hold can have two values, 0 or 1, a qubit can exist in a state called "superposition" of both basis states simultaneously. Using the Dirac "bra-ket" notation, the general state of a qubit is a unit vector in the two-dimensional complex Hilbert space $\mathbb{C}^2$, written as: 

\begin{equation*}
  \ket{\psi} = \alpha\ket{0} + \beta\ket{1},
  \qquad \alpha,\beta \in \mathbb{C}, \quad |\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1,
  \label{eq:qubit}
\end{equation*}

where $\ket{0}$ and $\ket{1}$ are the two computational basis states.

\subsubsection{Measurement and State Collapse}

Measuring a qubit in a chosen base collapses its state into one of the basis vectors. If the qubit is in the state  $\ket{\psi} = \alpha\ket{0} + \beta\ket{1}$ and we measure it in the computational basis $\{\ket{0}, \ket{1}\}$, the probability that the outcome will be 0 is $|\alpha|^2$, and for the outcome 1 it will be $|\beta|^2$ (the Born rule). After the measurement, the qubit is in the base-state corresponding to the observed outcome, while the original amplitudes $\alpha$, $\beta$ cannot be recovered.

If a qubit is prepared in one basis and then measured in a
different basis, the outcome is essentially random and offers no
additional information about the original state. For a concrete
example, let's consider a qubit prepared in the state $\ket{0}$ (a
Z-basis state) and then measured in the diagonal X-basis.
Rewriting $\ket{0}$ in the X-basis gives
\begin{equation*}
  \ket{0} = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\bigl(\ket{+} + \ket{-}\bigr),
\end{equation*}
so the measurement returns $\ket{+}$ with probability~$\tfrac{1}{2}$
and $\ket{-}$ with probability~$\tfrac{1}{2}$. The outcome carries
no information about the original Z-basis preparation, and the
qubit collapses irreversibly into the measured X-basis state.
The reverse situation (i.e. X-basis preparation, Z-basis
measurement) is analogous.

\subsubsection{Hadamard Gate and the Diagonal Basis}

In addition to the computational $Z$-basis $\{\ket{0},\ket{1}\}$, BB84 also uses the diagonal basis ($X$-basis) $\{\ket{+},\ket{-}\}$, defined by:
\begin{equation*}
  \ket{+} = \frac{\ket{0} + \ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}, \qquad
  \ket{-} = \frac{\ket{0} - \ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}.
  \label{eq:hadamard-states}
\end{equation*}

The two bases are incompatible: a qubit prepared as $\ket{0}$ or
$\ket{1}$ gives a random outcome when measured in $\{\ket{+}, \ket{-}\}$ and vice-versa.

The transformation between the two bases is performed by the
\textbf{Hadamard gate $H$}:
\begin{equation*}
  \quad H\ket{0} = \ket{+}, \quad H\ket{1} = \ket{-},
  \label{eq:hadamard}
\end{equation*}

described by the following matrix: \begin{equation*}
  H = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix}.
  \label{eq:hadamard}
\end{equation*}


A quantum gate transforms a qubit state, similar to a classical logic gate. As seen above, the Hadamard gate maps the computational basis into the diagonal basis

In the \textbf{Sequre} implementation, the Hadamard gate is applied in Qiskit via 'qc.h(0)' when Alice encodes a bit in the $X$-basis (diagonal). Bob applies the same Hadamard gate before a standard measurement in the computational basis, which is the equivalent to measuring directly in the diagonal basis.

\subsubsection{Pauli Gates}

The \textbf{Pauli gates} ($X$, $Y$, $Z$) are the 
single-qubit operations corresponding to the Pauli matrices:
\begin{equation*}
  X = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad
  Y = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad
  Z = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation*}

The $X$ gate is the quantum equivalent of the classical \textsc{not}
gate. The $Z$ gate
leaves $\ket{0}$ unchanged and flips the sign of $\ket{1}$, defining
the computational basis. The $Y$ gate combines both effects. In
the \textbf{Sequre} BB84 implementation, the $X$ gate is applied
explicitly via 'qc.x(0)' when Alice encodes a $1$ bit in the
$Z$-basis, while the $Z$ basis itself serves as the default
computational measurement basis (no $Z$-gate is applied; instead, measurement is performed directly in this basis).


\subsection{The No-Cloning Theorem}
\label{sec:no-cloning}

The \textbf{no-cloning theorem} (Wootters and Żurek,
1982~\cite{wootters1982}) states that no quantum operation can
produce an exact copy of an arbitrary quantum state. A short
algebraic argument illustrates this: if a unitary cloning operation existed, then for any two input states $\ket{\psi}$ and $\ket{\phi}$
the inner product would have to satisfy
$\braket{\psi|\phi} = \braket{\psi|\phi}^2$, an identity that holds
only when the states are either orthogonal or identical.


The consequence for QKD is direct. Eve cannot intercept a qubit, keep a perfect copy for herself and forward the original to Bob, unchanged. Any attempt to extract information from the channel forces her to interact with the qubit, and that interaction disturbs it, as the next section shows.


\subsection{Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle in QKD}
\label{sec:heisenberg}

The BB84 protocol rests on \textbf{Heisenberg's uncertainty principle}, which states that it is impossible to simultaneously know, with full precision, the outcome of measurements in two incompatible bases. This is a purely quantum-mechanical phenomenon, because in classical physics, any observable can in principle be measured arbitrarily precisely without disturbing the others. Applied to the $Z$-basis and the $X$-basis, this means that if Alice prepares a qubit in one basis and Eve measures it in the other, the outcome Eve will read is purely random, and the act of measuring collapses the qubit into a state that no longer encodes Alice's original bit. This is the mechanism by which eavesdropping is detected in BB84. 

\subsection{Quantum Entanglement}
\label{sec:entanglement}

An indispensable principle of quantum mechanics that QKD can use is
\textbf{quantum entanglement}: two particles can be prepared in a
common state such that measuring one instantly fixes the outcome of
the other, no matter how far apart they are. The correlation between
the two outcomes is stronger than anything classical pre-arrangement
can ever produce, and this gap is what the \textbf{E91} protocol
(section~\ref{sec:e91}) exploits to derive a shared key and to detect
an eavesdropper at the same time. 


\subsection{Quantum Bit Error Rate (QBER)}
\label{sec:qber}

The \textbf{Quantum Bit Error Rate} (QBER) is defined as the fraction of
sifted key bits on which Alice and Bob "disagree":
\begin{equation*}
  Q = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mathbf{1}[a_i \neq b_i],
  \label{eq:qber}
\end{equation*}
where $N$ is the length of the sifted key, $a_i$, $b_i$ are the
corresponding bits held by Alice and Bob after the sifting procedure and $\mathbf{1}[\dots]$ is the indicator function (equal to $1$ when the condition is true and $0$ otherwise).

In a noise-free channel without eavesdropping, every sifted bit satisfies  $b_i = a_i$, so $QBER = 0$. Real channels and detectors add noise, which raises $QBER$ by a small, bounded amount. An active intercept-resend attack raises $QBER$ significantly above any
noise floor. Specifically:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{No Eve, no noise:} $QBER \approx 0$.
  \item \textbf{Weak Eve}: (i.e. Eve intercepts a fraction $p$ of qubits): each intercepted
        qubit introduces an error with probability $\frac{1}{4}$ (wrong basis
        with the probability $\frac{1}{2}$, error given wrong basis with the probability
        $\frac{1}{2}$), so $QBER \approx \frac{p}{4}$. 
        ($p = 0.30$), this gives $QBER \approx 7.5\%$.
  \item \textbf{Strong Eve}: ($p = 1.0$, full intercept-resend): $QBER \approx 25\%$.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Sequre} uses a security threshold of $11\%$, consistent with
the standard BB84 security analysis~\cite{shorPreskill2000}: if $QBER \geq 11\%$,
the protocol is aborted and no key will be output. This threshold ensures that any
remaining information Eve holds about the key can be made exponentially small
with the help of privacy amplification.

\subsection{Error Correction \& Privacy Amplification}
\label{sec:ec-pa}

After sifting and the QBER verification, now Alice and Bob have two strings of bits which should match on most of the positions, but usually they do not match exactly: the noise within the channel produces a non-zero QBER even when there is no intercepting. The last two steps of BB84 reconcile the two keys into an identical key and then compress the result in order to eliminate any potential knowledge Eve may have gained

\subsection{The BB84 Protocol}
\label{sec:bb84}

As introduced in chapter~\ref{sec:introduction}, BB84~\cite{bb84} is
the first QKD protocol, proposed by Bennett and
Brassard in 1984, and involves the three actors \textbf{Alice}
(sender), \textbf{Bob} (receiver), and \textbf{Eve} (potential
eavesdropper). What distinguishes BB84 from classical key-exchanging methods is the environment itself: each key bit is encoded into the polarisation state of a single qubit, so any attempt by Eve to measure or copy said qubit disturbs it in a way the legitimate parties can detect by comparing a small subset of their results. The protocol produces a common secret key between Alice and Bob in
six steps. The first three establish a raw shared sequence of bits
over a quantum channel; the last three transform it into a final
secret key using a classical public channel. The steps are:

\paragraph{Step 1: Qubit preparation (Alice).}
Alice generates a random bit string $\mathbf{a} = (a_1, \ldots, a_n)$ and an
independent random basis string $\mathbf{x} = (x_1, \ldots, x_n)$, where each
$x$ is either \textit{X} or \textit{Z}. She encodes each bit $a_i$ into a qubit according to the table below and sends its the qubits one by one over the quantum channel.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \label{tab:bb84-states}
  \begin{tabular}{@{} cccl @{}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Bit} & \textbf{Basis} & \textbf{State} & \textbf{Description} \\
    \midrule
    0 & $Z$ (rectilinear) & $\ket{0}$ & spin-up / horizontal polarisation \\
    1 & $Z$ (rectilinear) & $\ket{1}$ & spin-down / vertical polarisation \\
    0 & $X$ (diagonal)    & $\ket{+} = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{0}+\ket{1})$ & $+45^\circ$ polarisation \\
    1 & $X$ (diagonal)    & $\ket{-} = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{0}-\ket{1})$ & $-45^\circ$ polarisation \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

In practice, a qubit is built like a physical system with two layers. The most common implementations are the spin of the electron ($\ket{0}$ = spin-up, $\ket{1}$ = spin-down) and the photon polarization ($\ket{0}$ = horizontal, $\ket{1}$ = vertical); most of the times, BB84 uses photons, while \textbf{Sequre} uses the abstract qubit model from Qiskit, without a specific physical implementation.

\paragraph{Step 2: Qubit measurement (Bob).}
Bob independently chooses a random basis string $\mathbf{y} = (y_1, \ldots, y_n)$
and measures each received qubit in the corresponding basis, recording his
outcomes as $\mathbf{b} = (b_1, \ldots, b_n)$.

\paragraph{Step 3: Basis sifting.}
Alice and Bob communicate their basis choices $\mathbf{x}$ and $\mathbf{y}$
over a classical public channel (no bit values are revealed). They retain
only the positions where $x_i = y_i$ (on average, 50\% of all transmitted
qubits), and then the retained bits form the \textbf{sifted key}. The term "sifting" expresses the idea of filtering: Alice and Bob "sift" all the 
positions in which the bases have not matched, keeping those positions.

\paragraph{Step 4: Error rate estimation (QBER).}
Alice and Bob sacrifice a random subset of their sifted key to estimate the
Quantum Bit Error Rate (QBER). If it exceeds a security threshold, they
abort the protocol (more detailed in the below section 2.6).

\paragraph{Step 5: Error correction.}
Alice and Bob apply an error-correction protocol over the public channel to
reconcile the remaining bits of their sifted keys, so that the two keys become identical.
(more in section~\ref{sec:ec-pa}).

\paragraph{Step 6: Privacy amplification.}
To account for any partial information Eve may have obtained, Alice and Bob
apply a universal hash function, SHA-256 to compress their reconciled key into a shorter final key, whose correlation with anything Eve would know is negligible. 
(more in section~\ref{sec:ec-pa}).


\subsubsection{Error Correction}

In \textbf{error correction} (also called information reconciliation), Alice and Bob communicate over the public classical channel to make their two keys match.  

\textbf{Sequre} uses a simple \textbf{parity-block scheme}: the sifted key 
is divided into consecutive blocks of 4 bits. For each block, Alice announces the parity (XOR of the 4 bits) on the public channel, and Bob compares it with his own. If both parties agree, they keep the block, but if they disagree, the block contains at least one error and is discarded by both of them. This scheme is simple to analyze, but inefficient. Why? Because it sacrifices an entire block for any single bit error. 

\subsubsection{Privacy Amplification}

After error correction, Alice and Bob share an identical key, but Eve may hold partial information about it: she observed every parity announcement on the public channel during the error correction phase, and she may have obtained information from any qubits she intercepted before the QBER check revealed her presence. 
\textbf{Privacy amplification} compresses the reconciled key with a hash function (specifically SHA-256, producing a key that's 256 bits long, but truncating it to 128 bits as the \textit{final key}), reducing Eve's information about the output key to a negligible quantity, regardless of how much she learned about the input~\cite{bennett1995pa}.


\subsection{The CASCADE Information-Reconciliation Algorithm}
\label{sec:cascade}

While the parity-block scheme discards an entire block on any disagreement regarding the bits, \textbf{CASCADE} locates and flips each incorrect bit individually via binary search on parities, keeping the rest of the key intact. 
The algorithm runs in several passes. The first pass uses a block size $B_1$, chosen from the estimated QBER, and each next pass doubles it. 

\begin{equation*}
  B_1 \;\approx\; \left\lceil \frac{0.73}{Q} \right\rceil,
  \qquad B_{i+1} = 2\,B_i.
  \label{eq:cascade-blocksize}
\end{equation*}

At the start of a pass, the key is randomly permuted and split into blocks of size $B_1$. Alice announces the parity of the block on the public channel, and for every block whose parity does not match with Bob's, the two apply a binary search: it splits the block into two halves, compare their parity, then it recursively descends into the half where the parities differ and it stops when the incorrect bit is identified and flipped. 

The key step is the \textbf{back-correction} (i.e. retroactive correction). Flipping a bit in $i$-th pass might invalidate the parity of a block from a previous pass that contained the same bit, revealing a second error that was hidden until now. That's why CASCADE recursively revisits earlier blocks. After several passes, it converges with high probability, leaking a number of parity bits, number that is close to the \textbf{Shannon lower bound} (the information-theoretic minimum number of parity bits required for reconciliation at the given QBER) ~\cite{martinez2015cascade}. That total leak of information is the exact quantity that will be subtracted during privacy amplification in order to obtain the final secret key length. 

\subsection{Optical Fibre Channel Attenuation}
\label{sec:channel-loss}

Real life implementations of the BB84 protocol carry the qubits as photons through optical fibre. On long distances, the dominant limit is not the noise, but the \textbf{photon attenuation} within the channel. For standard optical fibre, which is 1550 nanometers in wavelength, the survival probability of a transmitted photon follows the \textbf{Beer-Lambert} law:

\begin{equation*}
  \eta(L) = 10^{-\alpha L / 10}, \qquad \alpha \approx 0.2 \text{ dB/km},
  \label{eq:fibre-attenuation}
\end{equation*}

where $\eta(L)$ represents the probability that a photon will survive over a distance of $L$ kilometres and $\alpha$ is the attenuation coefficient of the optic fibre (took from the ITU-T~G.652~\cite{ITU-T-G652} standard). This means that only $\sim10\%$ of photons survive a 50km long link and $\sim1\%$ do so on a 100km. This exponential decay sets the fundamental rate-distance limit for direct BB84 transmission and is one of the main reasons real implementations rely on weak coherent pulses with decoy-state monitoring, as discussed in the next section.


\subsection{Decoy-State Method \& Photon Number Splitting Attack}
\label{sec:decoy-state}

QKD systems do not have single-photon sources. Instead, they use
attenuated lasers, otherwise called \textbf{weak coherent pulses} (WCP). A WCP
with mean photon number $\mu$ emits $n$ photons per pulse with Poisson
probability:

\begin{equation*}
  P(n \mid \mu) \;=\; e^{-\mu}\,\frac{\mu^n}{n!},
  \label{eq:poisson}
\end{equation*}

where $P(n \mid \mu)$ is the probability a pulse will contain exactly $n$ photons given an intensity $\mu$. For $\mu<1$ most pulses contain either no photons or one photon, but a small fraction of pulses contains $n\geq2$ photons. These multi-photon pulses make the PNS attack possible. Overall, the PNS attack is most dangerous over long distances, where channel loss suppresses the legitimate single-photon signal while multi-photon pulses pass through unaffected. 

The \textbf{Decoy-State Method}~\cite{lo2005decoy,wang2005decoy} defends against PNS attacks by having Alice randomly alternate between a signal intensity $\mu$ (used for the key) and one or more decoy intensities $\nu$, used for monitoring. Thus, Eve cannot distinguish in advance the signal pulses from the decoy pulses, so any selective suppression of single-photon pulses shows up as a discrepancy between the gains of the two intensities. The \textbf{gain} at intensity $\mu$ is the total detection rate $Q_\mu = \sum_n P(n \mid \mu)\,Y_n$, where $Y_n$ is the conditioned probability that Bob will detect a pulse containing exactly $n$ photons. Combining the two "gains" measured at the two intensities, the analysis will produce a lower bound (limit) on the single-photon yield $Y_1$:

\begin{equation*}
  Y_1^{L} \;\geq\;
    \frac{\mu}{\mu\nu - \nu^2}\!\left(
      Q_\nu e^{\nu} - Q_\mu e^{\mu}\,\frac{\nu^2}{\mu^2}
      - \frac{\mu^2 - \nu^2}{\mu^2}\,Y_0
    \right),
  \label{eq:y1-bound}
\end{equation*}
where $\mu$ and $\nu$ are the signal and decoy intensities respectively, $Q_\mu$ and $Q_\nu$ are the measured gains at each intensity, and $Y_0$ is the background noise of the so-called \textbf{dark counts} (i.e. detections triggered by the detector noise. $Y_1^L$ is the central quantity: it lower-bounds the rate at which genuine single-photon events reach Bob, and the secure key rate is then computed from $Y_1^L$ via the \textbf{GLLP formula} ~\cite{gllp2004}. Sequre's decoy-state panel renders $Q_\mu$, $Q_\nu$, $Y_1^L$ and the resulting key rate live, so the user can watch the single-photon contribution being recovered from the WCP statistics in real time.

This formula is named after Daniel Gottesman, Hoi-Kwong Lo,
Norbert Lütkenhaus, and John Preskill (2004), who proved the
asymptotic security bound for BB84 with imperfect devices
(weak coherent pulse sources, lossy channels, and noisy detectors). It takes the single-photon yield $Y_1^L$, the single-photon error rate, and the measured QBER as inputs and returns the number of secure key bits Alice and Bob can extract per pulse after subtracting both the error-correction leakage and the privacy-amplification overhead.



\subsection{Machine Learning for QKD Security Monitoring}
\label{sec:ml-detection}
\label{sec:lstm-impl}

The QBER threshold check catches any adversary that exceeds the
$11\%$ abort threshold, but it is blind by design to an attacker
that purposefully stays below it (i.e. a smart Eve, see section~\ref{sec:eve-impl}). To detect this case, \textbf{Sequre}
adds a complementary layer of analysis: a binary classifier that
takes the output of one BB84 run and returns the estimated
probability that Eve was present.

Eavesdropping detection in QKD is a manner of binary classification
problem: given the output of a BB84 run, decide whether Eve was
present. Many machine learning algorithms can solve it: classical
methods like \textbf{logistic regression}, \textbf{support vector
machines}, or \textbf{gradient-boosted trees} work on
fixed-length feature vectors, while deep models such as
feed-forward networks, one-dimensional convolutional networks,
and transformer-based sequence models learn patterns directly
from the per-qubit sequence. \textbf{Sequre} implements one
algorithm from each family: \textbf{Random Forest} for fast,
interpretable verdicts from post-run features, and an LSTM that
reads the per-qubit sequence position by position.


The \textbf{Random Forest}~\cite{breiman2001} works by training many
decision trees on different random subsets of the data and
combining their votes into a single prediction. \textbf{Sequre}
uses it for two practical reasons. Firstly, it reports which
inputs were most influential in each decision through
feature-importance scores. Secondly, it works well on small
datasets, the kinds that accumulate during a single user
session, without needing a large precomputed training set. In
\textbf{Sequre}, the input is a vector of six numbers measured
after the run finishes: the QBER, the sifting efficiency, the
configured channel distance, the depolarising-noise probability,
the measurement-error probability, and the sifted-key length.
The output is the estimated probability that Eve was present,
$\hat{P}(\text{Eve}) \in [0, 1]$, together with a list of how
much each input contributed to that probability. The model is
retrained from the user's own simulation history, which is stored
locally, so it adapts to the runs the user has actually performed rather than to a fixed external dataset.


The \textbf{Long Short-Term Memory} (LSTM) network~\cite{hochreiter1997} is a type of neural network designed for sequences. It reads inputs one at a time, in order, and keeps an internal memory that lets it remember what it saw
earlier in the sequence. \textbf{Sequre} uses it for the per-qubit task because the intercept-resend attack leaves a temporal pattern: errors cluster on basis-matched positions where Eve picked the wrong basis, while basis-mismatched positions stay random regardless of whether Eve is there. The Random Forest cannot see this pattern because it only receives summary statistics, whereas the LSTM reads the qubits one at a time and can pick up the pattern directly. At each position it reads Alice's basis, Alice's bit, Bob's basis, Bob's outcome, and the basis-matched flag, then updates its internal memory. The model is trained on a synthetic dataset of labelled runs (with Eve present or absent at different interception rates), and after training it returns a single probability that Eve was present in the current run, $P(\text{Eve} \mid \text{sequence})$.


Together the two detectors form a layered defence: the Random
Forest provides a fast, interpretable verdict from coarse
features, while the LSTM provides a sharper signal in the
sub-threshold regime where the Random Forest would miss the
attack. The empirical performance of both models is reported in
Section~\ref{sec:lstm-results}.


\subsection{Alternative QKD Protocols}
\label{sec:alt-protocols}

\textbf{Sequre} also implements another three QKD protocols, which are \textbf{B92}, \textbf{SARG04} and \textbf{E91}, the first two being closely related to BB84. In short, B92 simplifies its encoding, while SARG04 modifies its public announcement. E91 takes a completely different route, deriving its security from \textit{quantum entanglement} rather than disturbance under measurement

\subsubsection{B92 (Bennett, 1992)}
\label{sec:b92}

The B92 protocol~\cite{b92} was proposed by none other than \textbf{Charles Bennett}, the first half of the pair that developed the BB84 protocol. It proves that we can make QKD only using two quantum states, one for each base. It simplifies BB84 to just two non-orthogonal states: Alice sends $\ket{0}$ for bit 0 and $\ket{+}$ for bit 1. Bob measures randomly in the Z or X bases and keeps only conclusive outcomes (i.e. those that are incompatible with one of Alice's two possible states). Its disadvantages are that about $25\%$ of qubits survive sifting (compared to $50\%$ in BB84) and the protocol is more vulnerable to the noise within the optical fibre and less efficient in practice than BB84, thus being rarely used in modern-day commercial systems.

\subsubsection{SARG04 (Scarani-Acín-Ribordy-Gisin, 2004)}
\label{sec:sarg04}

SARG04~\cite{sarg04} was created to counter the PNS attacks, those where Eve "steals" a photon from the pulses that mistakenly contain more than one photon. SARG04 uses the same four qubit states as BB84 but it changes the manner of the public announcement: instead of revealing her base, Alice announces an unordered pair of non-orthogonal states, one of which is the one she sent. Bob retains the bit only if his outcome is consistent with one of the two. Since Eve cannot tell which state was sent without measuring first, SARG04 resists the PNS attack more effectively than BB84 does, at the cost of $\approx 25\%$ sifting efficiency.

\subsubsection{E91 (Ekert, 1991) and the CHSH Bell Test}
\label{sec:e91}
 
While BB84 derives its security from the no-cloning theorem and measurement disturbance, E91~\cite{ekert1991} derives it from \textbf{quantum entanglement}.  A central source emits pairs of photons in the
entangled \textbf{Bell state} $\ket{\Phi^+}$, one of four maximally
entangled two-qubit states named after John Bell:

\begin{equation*}
  \ket{\Phi^+} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\bigl(\ket{00} + \ket{11}\bigr),
  \label{eq:bell-state}
\end{equation*}

which couples the two particles such that a measurement on one instantly fixes the outcome of the other. The source sends one photon to Alice and one to Bob, both measuring their photon at a randomly chosen orientation (basis) from a fixed set of three: 
\begin{align*}
  \text{Alice:} &\quad a_1 = 0^\circ,\; a_2 = 45^\circ,\; a_3 = 90^\circ, \\
  \text{Bob:}   &\quad b_1 = 45^\circ,\; b_2 = 90^\circ,\; b_3 = 135^\circ.
  \label{eq:e91-angles}
\end{align*}
Within the E91 protocol, angles are strategically chosen at intervals of 45 degrees, because at differences of $45^\circ$ and $135^\circ$, the trigonometric functions \textit{sin} and \textit{cos} that govern entangled photons reach values of $\pm\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \approx \pm0.707$. When their bases match ($a_2 = b_1 = 45^\circ$ or $a_3 = b_2 = 90^\circ$), the outcomes are perfectly correlated and form the raw key. When the bases differ, the measurement results fuel the \textbf{CHSH correlator} $S$ (Clauser, Horne, Shimony, Holt, 1969), a numerical version of the Bell inequality that distinguishes the classical pre-established correlations from the actually quantum-entangled ones:
\begin{equation*}
  S = E(a, b) - E(a, b') + E(a', b) + E(a', b'),
  \label{eq:chsh}
\end{equation*}
where $a, a'$ are two of Alice's orientations, $b, b'$ are two of
Bob's, and
$E(\theta_a, \theta_b) = P(\text{same} \mid \theta_a, \theta_b)
- P(\text{different} \mid \theta_a, \theta_b)$
is the correlation between their outcomes at those orientations.

Concretely, $S$ is bounded at $|S| \leq 2$ for any classical correlations and can reach $|S| = 2\sqrt{2}$ (the Tsirelson limit) for ideal quantum-entanglement. Eavesdropping breaks the entanglement and drives $|S|$ towards the classical bound, but under strong Eve, $S$ collapses to almost 0. In the end, \textbf{Sequre} computes $S$ after every E91 run and plots it between the two bounds.

\subsection{Finite Key Security Bounds}
\label{sec:finite-key-theory}

The standard BB84 security proof assumes an infinite key length. In practice, any real run uses a finite block of $n$ sifted bits and the measured QBER ($\hat{Q}$) fluctuates by an amount of order $1/\sqrt{n}$. A rigorous analysis with finite keys must keep this fluctuation in mind. 

The reference analysis is given by \textbf{Marco Tomamichel} and his collaborators~\cite{tomamichel2012}, reference that is the standard on tight finite key bounds in QKD. They correct the measured QBER through a Hoeffding bound:

\begin{equation*}
  Q_\mathrm{upper} = \hat{Q} + \delta_\mathrm{PE}, \qquad
  \delta_\mathrm{PE} = \sqrt{\frac{\ln(2/\varepsilon_\mathrm{PE})}{2n}},
  \label{eq:hoeffding}
\end{equation*}

where $\delta_\mathrm{PE}$ is a small statistical penalty (or safety margin, of order
$1/\sqrt{n}$) added to $\hat{Q}$ to obtain a worst-case scenario QBER
$Q_\mathrm{upper}$, and $\varepsilon_\mathrm{PE}$ is the chosen
confidence parameter. The resulting key length is bounded by:

\begin{equation*}
  \ell \leq n\bigl[1 - H_2(Q_\mathrm{upper})\bigr]
           - n\,f_\mathrm{EC}\,H_2(\hat{Q})
           - \log_2\!\tfrac{2}{\varepsilon_\mathrm{corr}}
           - 2\log_2\!\tfrac{1}{2\varepsilon_\mathrm{PA}},
  \label{eq:finite-key}
\end{equation*}

where $H_2$ is the binary entropy, $f_\mathrm{EC} \approx 1.16$ is
the CASCADE efficiency, and the four terms are (in order) the
asymptotic key gain, the error-correction leakage, and the
correctness and privacy-amplification overheads, controlled by the
small failure probabilities $\varepsilon_\mathrm{corr}$ and
$\varepsilon_\mathrm{PA}$.

\textbf{Sequre}'s 'Finite Key' panel evaluates this bound live and flags
whether the current block size yields a positive key length or not.

\section{Existing QKD Simulators and Related Work}
\label{sec:related-work}

Several software platforms exist for simulating QKD. This chapter surveys the most widely used tools, evaluates their scope and limitations and positions \textbf{Sequre} relative to the state of the art.


\subsection{NetSquid}
\label{sec:netsquid}

NetSquid~\cite{netsquid2021} (Network Simulator for Quantum Information using
Discrete events) is a high-fidelity, discrete-event simulator for quantum
networks. Its primary design goal is performance characterisation of
quantum repeater networks, entanglement distribution protocols, and
quantum memory systems, not QKD education. It models physical processes
(photon propagation, decoherence, detector timing jitter, dark counts) at a
component level of detail that greatly exceeds what is needed for
demonstrating BB84. As a consequence, NetSquid requires substantial
domain expertise to use, has no browser-accessible interface, and is not
suited to interactive classroom demonstration. It also does not integrate
with real IBM Quantum hardware.

\subsection{QuNetSim and SimulaQron}
\label{sec:qunetsim}

QuNetSim~\cite{qunetsim2021} and SimulaQron~\cite{simulaqron2018} are
quantum-network simulation frameworks that provide an abstraction layer
for writing distributed quantum protocols in Python. Both can simulate
QKD as a special case but are designed as generic network-layer toolkits
rather than QKD-specific platforms. They expose low-level primitives
(qubit send/receive, teleportation, entanglement swapping) rather than
a pedagogically structured BB84 pipeline. Neither provides real-time
visualisation, a web interface, or integration with cloud quantum hardware.

\subsection{Qiskit Textbook Demos}
\label{sec:qiskit-textbook}

IBM's Qiskit learning platform includes interactive Jupyter-notebook
demonstrations of BB84 that use Qiskit circuits and Aer simulation.
These notebooks are closest in spirit to \textbf{Sequre}: they use the same
Qiskit circuit model, support noise injection, and are intended for
students. They are however static notebooks rather than a deployable
web application, and do not persist results, stream events live,
integrate with hardware via a polished interface, or offer adversarial
simulation.

\newpage
\section{Technologies Used}
\label{sec:technologies}

This chapter describes the core technologies that underpin the \textbf{Sequre}
platform, covering both the backend computation stack and the frontend
presentation layer.

\subsection{Python 3.11}
\label{sec:python}

The backend is written in Python 3.11. Python was chosen for its mature
ecosystem of quantum-computing libraries (Qiskit, PyTorch), scientific
computing packages (NumPy), and web frameworks (FastAPI). The 3.11 release
specifically was selected for its 10--25\% speed improvement over Python 3.9
in CPU-bound loops, which is measurable during the per-qubit simulation
loop at larger qubit counts.

\subsection{Qiskit \& Qiskit-Aer}
\label{sec:qiskit}

\textbf{Qiskit}~\cite{qiskit2024} is IBM's open-source SDK for quantum
computing. \textbf{Sequre} uses it to construct per-qubit BB84 circuits: Alice's
encoding gate sequence (optional X gate for bit~1, optional H gate for
the $X$-basis) and Bob's measurement in the chosen basis. The circuit
for a single qubit exchange is a one-qubit circuit of depth at most~3,
kept intentionally shallow to minimise transpilation overhead when
dispatching to real hardware.

\textbf{Qiskit-Aer} is the high-performance simulator backend. It supports
both ideal statevector simulation (used for the zero-noise baseline) and
noisy simulation via the NoiseModel API. \textbf{Sequre} builds a noise
model with depolarising errors on the X and H gates and a Pauli
bit-flip channel on measurement, parameterised by the user-controlled
'depolarizing\_prob' and 'measurement\_error\_prob' inputs.

\subsection{Qiskit IBM Runtime}
\label{sec:ibm-runtime}

For real-hardware execution, \textbf{Sequre} integrates with the
\textbf{Qiskit IBM Runtime} SamplerV2 API. Individual BB84
circuits are batched into a single SamplerV2 job, dispatched
asynchronously to a configurable IBM Quantum backend
(e.g. 'ibm\_fez', 'ibm\_marrakesh', 'ibm\_kingston' or any other device accessible to the configured IBM). Results are streamed to the
frontend over the open WebSocket connection as soon as the job
completes. Access credentials are stored in a server-side '.env'
file that is never committed to version control.

\subsection{FastAPI}
\label{sec:fastapi}

FastAPI was mainly chosen for three reasons that mattered to my use case. First, its native \texttt{async/await} support made it
straightforward to stream per-qubit events over a WebSocket without
blocking the event loop on the Qiskit simulation calls (achieved via
\texttt{asyncio.sleep(0)} yield points). Second, its automatic
OpenAPI documentation (available at \texttt{/docs}) documents all
REST endpoints without additional effort. Third, its Pydantic integration
provides input validation and serialisation for all request and response
models.

\textbf{Sequre}'s FastAPI application exposes two types of endpoints: REST
endpoints (simulation history, ML training, authentication, challenge
progress) under \texttt{/api/v1/}, and a single WebSocket endpoint at
\texttt{/ws/simulate/\{session\_id\}} that drives the live simulation
stream.

\subsection{WebSockets for Real-Time Streaming}
\label{sec:websockets}

The WebSocket protocol provides a full-duplex, persistent TCP connection
over which the server can push events to the client without polling. In
\textbf{Sequre}, the frontend opens a WebSocket to
\texttt{/ws/simulate/\{uuid\}}, sends the simulation configuration as a
JSON object, and then receives a stream of \texttt{type: "qubit"} events
(one per simulated qubit, carrying Alice's basis and bit, Bob's basis
and result, and the Eve-intercept flag) followed by a single
\texttt{type: "result"} event containing the full summary. This streaming
architecture is the technical foundation of the PhotonGrid animation and
the progressive QBER update.

\subsection{SQLite and SQLAlchemy}
\label{sec:sqlite}

Simulation runs, user accounts, password-reset tokens, and Challenge Mode
attempts are persisted in a \textbf{SQLite} database
('sequre.db') via the \textbf{SQLAlchemy} ORM. SQLite was selected
over a client-server database (e.g.\ PostgreSQL) because \textbf{Sequre} is
designed for single-host deployment; the operational write rate (one
INSERT per simulation run) is well within SQLite's concurrency limits.
SQLAlchemy provides declarative model definitions and a session-based
transaction API; schema migrations are handled via idempotent
"ALTER TABLE" statements executed at startup, which add new
columns if absent and silently skip them otherwise.

\subsection{PyTorch}
\label{sec:pytorch}

\textbf{PyTorch} is used for the LSTM eavesdropper detector
(section~\ref{sec:lstm-impl}). The CPU build (no CUDA required) is
installed as a backend dependency. The LSTM, linear classifier, and
training loop are implemented in 'backend/ml/lstm\_detector.py';
the trained weights are saved to 'lstm\_weights.pt' at the end
of the first training run and loaded from disk on subsequent server
starts. The entire training cycle (1\,200 synthetic sequences, 60 epochs)
completes in under 30 seconds on a modern CPU.

\subsection{React 19 and Vite}
\label{sec:react}

The frontend is a \textbf{React 19} single-page application bundled with
\textbf{Vite}. React's component model allows each visualisation panel
(PhotonGrid, DecoyStatePanel, FiniteKeyPanel, LstmDetectionPanel, etc.)
to be a self-contained, props-driven component that re-renders only when
its data changes. Vite provides sub-second hot-module replacement during
development and a highly optimised production bundle.

Client-side routing between the main Dashboard (\texttt{/}) and the
Challenge Mode page (\texttt{/challenge}) is provided by
\textbf{React Router DOM v7}, which intercepts URL changes and renders the
appropriate page component without a full-page reload.

\subsection{Tailwind CSS}
\label{sec:tailwind}

\textbf{Tailwind CSS} is a utility-first CSS framework that generates
a minimal stylesheet containing only the classes actually used in the
project. \textbf{Sequre} uses Tailwind exclusively for styling: all component
layout, colour, spacing, and responsive behaviour is expressed via
inline class names. The dark-mode design (gray-950 background, violet
accent) is achieved with Tailwind's colour palette without any custom
CSS files.

\subsection{Recharts}
\label{sec:recharts}

The QBER historical chart and the key-rate-vs-distance curve are rendered
with \textbf{Recharts}, a composable charting library built on top of
D3 and React. It was chosen for its declarative API and out-of-the-box
responsiveness, which eliminates the need for manual SVG manipulation.

\subsection{Authentication Stack}
\label{sec:auth-stack}

The authentication subsystem uses:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{bcrypt} for password hashing (work factor~12);
  \item \textbf{python-jose} for JWT access and refresh token signing
        (HS256, configurable secret);
  \item \textbf{Starlette 'SessionMiddleware'} for the OAuth
        \texttt{state} parameter across the Google redirect;
  \item \textbf{Authlib} for the Google OAuth 2.0 flow
        (authorisation-code with PKCE);
  \item \textbf{HttpOnly, SameSite=Lax} cookie delivery of the
        access token, combined with a CSRF double-submit cookie for
        state-changing requests.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Sequre} follows a classic three-tier web architecture: a \textbf{React SPA}
frontend served by Vite (development) or a static file server (production),
a \textbf{FastAPI} backend that exposes REST and WebSocket endpoints, and a
\textbf{SQLite} database for persistence. The backend communicates outwards to the IBM Quantum Runtime API for hardware execution. The frontend additionally performs an unauthenticated geolocation lookup to \texttt{ipapi.co} for the session metadata widget, lookup that does not transit the backend.

\subsection{High-Level System Overview}

development, served from \texttt{/} by the backend's static-file middleware in
production) and the backend (port~8000) run on the same machine. Vite's
development proxy forwards all \texttt{/api/v1/} and \texttt{/ws/} requests
to the FastAPI process, so the browser sees a single origin with no
cross-origin restrictions. A \texttt{.env} file at the backend root holds all
secrets (JWT key, IBM token, Google OAuth credentials) and is excluded from
version control.

\subsection{Request and Event Flow}
\label{sec:request-flow}

A complete simulation cycle proceeds as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item The user configures parameters in 'SimulationControls' and
        presses \emph{Run Simulation}.
  \item The 'useSimulationSocket' hook opens a WebSocket to
        \texttt{/ws/simulate/\{uuid\}} and sends the configuration JSON.
  \item The FastAPI handler authenticates the optional session cookie,
        reads the configuration, and dispatches to the appropriate
        simulation path ('\_run\_bb84\_path' or
        '\_run\_alt\_protocol').
  \item For each qubit, the handler simulates the quantum circuit, applies
        Eve's interception logic, detector imperfections, and channel loss,
        then emits a \texttt{type: "qubit"} event via the WebSocket session
        manager.
  \item The frontend accumulates qubit events, updating \texttt{result}
        state in real time; the 'PhotonGrid' re-renders on each
        event.
  \item After all qubits, the handler runs classical post-processing
        (sifting, QBER, EC, PA, decoy analysis, finite key bounds, ML
        inference), persists the 'SimulationRun' row, and emits a
        \texttt{type: "result"} event with the full summary.
  \item The frontend populates all result panels from the summary object.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The BB84 Protocol Engine}
\label{sec:bb84-engine}

The core simulation loop in 'api/websocket\_routes.py' processes
qubits sequentially:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Alice's bits and bases are drawn from 'numpy.random.default\_rng'.
  \item Bob's bases are drawn independently.
  \item If WCP source: 'simulate\_wcp\_pulses()' draws per-pulse
        photon numbers from $\mathrm{Poisson}(\mu)$, applies channel
        transmittance, and returns a 'PulseResult' list. If PNS Eve
        is active, 'apply\_pns\_attack()' modifies the list in-place.
  \item If ideal source with channel distance: 'apply\_channel\_loss()'
        removes qubits that did not survive.
  \item For each surviving qubit, a Qiskit circuit is run on Aer with the
        configured noise model; Eve's interception logic is applied
        post-simulation if active.
  \item A \texttt{type: "qubit"} event is pushed over the WebSocket.
  \item After all qubits: sifting, QBER, EC, PA, decoy analysis,
        finite key bounds, RF and LSTM inference, and a
        \texttt{type: "result"} event.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Eve Eavesdropping Simulation}
\label{sec:eve-impl}

Four distinct adversary models are implemented:

\begin{description}
  \item[\textbf{Weak Eve} (30\%)]: Randomly intercepts 30\% of qubits
        with intercept-resend. Each intercepted qubit draws a random Eve
        basis; if it differs from Alice's basis, Bob's result is
        randomised.
  \item[\textbf{Strong Eve} (100\%)]: Same logic at 100\% interception
        rate, producing QBER $\approx 25\%$.
  \item[\textbf{Smart Eve}]: An adaptive controller
        ('quantum\_logic/smart\_eve.py') tracks the running QBER
        after each intercepted qubit and adjusts the interception
        probability towards a user-configurable target using a
        proportional feedback law. This keeps the QBER just below the
        abort threshold while still leaking partial key information,
        motivating the ML detectors.
  \item[\textbf{PNS Eve}]: Applied to the WCP pulse list before circuit
        simulation. Vacuum pulses ($n=0$) are unchanged; single-photon
        pulses ($n=1$) are blocked (set \texttt{detected=False}); multiphoton
        pulses ($n \geq 2$) are split (Eve retains one photon,
        \texttt{n\_survived = n-1} forwarded). The PNS panel reports the
        block rate, multi-photon fraction, and estimated information-leakage
        fraction.
\end{description}

The 15 missions are grouped into five difficulty bands, as follows:

\begin{description}

  \item[Levels 13--15 (Engineer, hard)] Long-distance runs with PNS and

(\texttt{>=}, \texttt{==}, \texttt{<}). The score scales linearly with
the fraction of constraints satisfied.

\subsection{B92 Sifting Efficiency}
\label{sec:b92-results}

The B92 preset scenario (600 qubits, clean channel) was run 20 times.
The mean sifting efficiency was $25.7 \pm 1.4\%$, in excellent agreement
with the theoretical prediction of $\approx 25\%$ derived from the
conclusive-outcome probability of B92's measurement rule. Under identical
conditions, BB84 achieved $49.8 \pm 1.2\%$ sifting efficiency, which is close
to the theoretical $50\%$.

\subsection{E91 CHSH Parameter}
\label{sec:e91-results}

The E91 Bell-test preset (1\,000 qubits, $p_\mathrm{dep} = 0.5\%$,
$p_\mathrm{meas} = 1.5\%$) was run 20 times. The mean CHSH parameter
was $S = 2.71 \pm 0.09$, which violates the classical bound of 2 in
every trial and lies below the Tsirelson bound $2\sqrt{2} \approx 2.828$
due to residual gate and measurement noise. Running the same preset under
Strong Eve collapsed $S$ to $0.08 \pm 0.12$, confirming that
entanglement destruction is reliably detected.

\subsection{LSTM Detector Performance}
\label{sec:lstm-results}

The LSTM eavesdropper detector (section~\ref{sec:lstm-impl}) achieves:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Validation accuracy: 92.0\%
  \item Training accuracy: 89.5\%
  \item Validation cross-entropy loss: 0.181
\end{itemize}

At the operational Weak Eve rate of 30\% (QBER $\approx 7.5\%$, below
the 11\% abort threshold), the LSTM returns $P(\text{Eve}) > 0.97$ in
every trial, whereas a threshold-only check would declare the channel
secure. False positive rate (no Eve declared Eve) over 200 honest runs
was 3.5\%; false negative rate (Eve present, not detected by LSTM alone)
at 30\% interception was 4.0\%.
For the LSTM panel, see section~\ref{sec:ml-panels}

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

\subsection{General Achievements}
\label{sec:achievements}

This thesis presented \textbf{Sequre}, a real-time simulation and
visualisation platform for quantum key distribution. The following
contributions were delivered:

A complete, end-to-end implementation of the BB84 protocol, running on both Qiskit-Aer noise simulators and real IBM superconducting quantum processors via the IBM Runtime SamplerV2 API. Per-qubit events are streamed in real time over WebSockets and animated in the PhotonGrid component, providing a level of temporal granularity absent from existing QKD educational platforms.

Three layers of analytical depth beyond the core protocol: a configurable
optical-fibre attenuation model, a three-intensity decoy-state analysis with
GLLP bounds, and a composable finite key security analysis following
Tomamichel.~\cite{tomamichel2012}. As a whole, these bring the
simulator to the level of a simplified research-grade testbed.

A spectrum of five adversary models (None, Weak, Strong, Smart and PNS)
that cover the full range from textbook intercept-resend to a calibrated
sub-threshold attacker and a silent multi-photon exploit, motivating the
two-layer ML detection stack.

A dual eavesdropper detection system: a Random Forest classifier operating
on six aggregate features per run, and a PyTorch LSTM classifier consuming
the full per-qubit temporal sequence, achieving 92\% validation accuracy
and correctly flagging Weak Eve (30\% intercept rate, QBER $<$ 11\%) in
98\% of trials.

Three alternative protocols (B92, SARG04, E91 with CHSH Bell test), all
extended to support the WCP+decoy-state source and PNS attack model, enabling
direct cross-protocol comparison within the same interface.

A gamified Challenge Mode with 15 procedurally parameterised missions,
two mission formats (\textbf{Detective} and \textbf{Engineer}), a global XP leaderboard,
per-user progress tracking, and post-failure pedagogical hints, is implemented
as a standalone authenticated feature that reuses the entire simulation stack.

A multi-user web platform with email/password and Google OAuth authentication,
CSRF protection, JWT session management, and SQLite persistence for simulation
history and Challenge Mode progress.

\newpage
\subsection{Future Development Possibilities}
\label{sec:future-work}

Several directions for future work are identified:

\textbf{Multiplayer adversarial mode.} The natural extension of Challenge
Mode is a 1v1 adversarial game where one player acts as Eve
(configuring the attack) and the other as Alice/Bob (declaring a verdict).
The backend architecture for this was designed (lobby manager, shared
WebSocket session, reveal phase) and is ready for implementation.

\textbf{Quantum repeater network visualisation.} The current rate--distance
chart shows the single-hop limit. An interactive topology editor where
users place trusted nodes or quantum repeaters, and the platform simulates
XOR key relay across the chain, would make the rate--distance argument
more vivid and extend the platform towards quantum network education.

\textbf{Continuous-variable QKD (CV-QKD).} Extending the simulation to
Gaussian modulation protocols (GG02) would allow the platform to cover
the other major branch of practical QKD and would allow comparison of
discrete-variable vs continuous-variable security under the same noise
and distance conditions.

\textbf{Measurement-device-independent (MDI-QKD).} Adding an MDI
protocol mode would eliminate the trusted-detector assumption and allow
the platform to demonstrate detector-blinding-resistant QKD, directly
addressing the class of attacks demonstrated by Makarov in 2010.

\textbf{Full device-independent (DI-QKD) via loophole-free Bell test.}
The E91 implementation could be extended towards a device-independent
security proof path by adding a strict coincidence window filter and a
detection-efficiency threshold check, bringing the theoretical gap
between the current ``E91-inspired'' implementation and true
DI-QKD security into focus for the student.

\textbf{Extended ML detection.} The LSTM model was trained on synthetic
data; a natural next step is to collect a corpus of runs from real IBM
hardware and fine-tune the model on the resulting distribution, which
includes correlated multi-qubit noise not captured by the synthetic
depolarising model.

\subsection{Closing Remarks}
\label{sec:closing}

Building \textbf{Sequre} over the past few months taught me something I did not expect:
the hardest part was not the quantum physics or the React UI, but tying
the two together in a way that streams qubit by qubit without lying to the
student. Watching the photon grid fill in cell by cell for the first time,
with Strong Eve causing exactly the $25\%$ QBER the theory predicts, was
the moment \textbf{Sequre} stopped being a checklist of features and became
something I wanted to actually keep building.

\textbf{Sequre} demonstrates that research-grade QKD analysis (i.e. composable finite key bounds, decoy-state security, LSTM eavesdropper detection, Bell-inequality certification) can be delivered in an interactive, browser-accessible shape accessible to students with no prior quantum computing experience. The modular architecture has proven to be effective: every major extension (alternative protocols, PNS attack, finite key, LSTM, Challenge Mode) was added without restructuring the existing simulation or post-processing pipeline. The platform is intended to remain in active development beyond this thesis, with the multiplayer adversarial mode being the next milestone.
